Quindío is the smallest of Colombia's coffee departments by area, but punches well above its weight. Centered on Armenia in the heart of the Eje Cafetero, it's where Jairo Arcila spent forty years as a mill manager at one of the country's largest exporters before retiring to focus on his own farms — Esmeralda, Maracay, Buenos Aires — and his sons' company Cofinet.

Quindío grows primarily Caturra and Castillo at 1,400 to 2,000 meters; the Arcilas were also among the first ten Colombian producers to plant Gesha, in 2014, and have helped popularize the Chiroso variety. The region's processing aesthetic tends to be more restrained than Cauca's — Ice fermentation and longer washed dwells more than thermal shock.
