Diego Samuel Bermudez and his brother Alex run Finca El Paraíso, outside Piendamó in the southern Colombian department of Cauca, the way most people would run a small chemistry lab. Coffee cherries arrive from the hillside; they are washed, then ozone-sterilized; they are inoculated with specific microbial cultures and held in stainless-steel bioreactors that monitor temperature, pH, and sugar concentration in real time. After several days of fermentation under tightly controlled conditions, the cherries get hit with what the Bermudezes call "thermal shock": a sequence of alternating hot and cold water rinses designed to lock in the volatile aromatics produced by the microbial activity. The resulting parchment is dried slowly. The resulting cup is — depending on the lot — peach, lychee, jasmine, and several other things you don't usually associate with coffee.

Diego founded the operation in 2008 with Alex. The brothers had grown up on the farm; they were rebuilding it, and looking for what would make their lots stand out in a market where most Colombian coffee gets sold blind into a commodity stream. The early experiments — washed Castillo, then naturals, then anaerobic ferments — were good; the bioreactor and thermal-shock work that followed was, by the late 2010s, unprecedented. Bermudez lots have since set Colombian auction records.

The names of those lots have become a small genre. *Lychee Peach* (a Castillo), *Lychee* (Wush Wush), *Letty* (a Gesha named for a family member): they describe the cup more than the coffee, and they signal that the producer is staking everything on what the fermentation does, not on the variety. This is a deliberate inversion of the standard specialty narrative, where a rare variety carries the story.

The Hachi Coffee Project, which Diego and Alex co-founded with other Cauca producers, is partly a way to scale that intensity without scaling the farm: rather than growing more coffee themselves, they apply their processing techniques to lots from other producers in the region, with attribution split. It is also, depending on who you ask, either a genuine attempt to lift the regional ecosystem or a vehicle for laundering the Bermudez brand into an indistinct collective.

Bermudez is the most controversial Colombian producer of the moment. A Hachi-affiliated lot entered into Best of Panama was disqualified after allegations of DNA alterations to the coffee — the precise nature of the alleged manipulation has not been publicly confirmed by the competition, but the disqualification was real, and it set off a real argument in the community about where exactly the line is between innovation and cheating. Diego has remained mostly quiet on the matter. The work continues.

His primary importer is The Coffee Quest, which moves most of his volume into the U.S. and Europe. The farm sits only a few kilometers from Wilton Benitez's Granja Paraíso 92, and the two producers are alternately framed as rivals or as the two leads of a single regional school. From the outside they're the same story: chemists who happen to grow coffee.
