Diego Samuel Bermudez and his older brother Alex run Finca El Paraíso, outside Piendamó in the southern Colombian department of Cauca, the way most people would run a small chemistry lab. Coffee cherries arrive from the hillside; they are washed, then ozone-sterilized; they are inoculated with specific microorganism strains — usually *Lactobacillus*, but the lineup rotates — and dropped into stainless-steel bioreactors that monitor temperature, sugar concentration, and pH in real time. After several days of fermentation under tightly controlled conditions, the cherries get hit with what the Bermudezes call "thermal shock": a sequence of alternating hot and cold water rinses, often jumping from 40°C to 12°C, designed to lock in the volatile aromatics produced by the microbial activity. The resulting parchment is dried slowly. The resulting cup is — depending on the lot — peach, lychee, jasmine, watermelon Jolly Rancher, and several other things you don't usually associate with coffee.

Diego started farming in 2008 with no formal training in either microbiology or coffee. The brothers had grown up on the farm; they were rebuilding it, and looking for what would make their lots stand out in a market where most Colombian coffee gets sold blind into a commodity stream. The early experiments — washed Castillo, then naturals, then anaerobic ferments — were good; the bioreactor and thermal-shock work, which started gaining real attention around 2018-2019, was unprecedented. By 2021 Bermudez lots were setting auction records, and by 2023 the "Lychee" Wush Wush was being bought for prices typically reserved for Geshas from Hacienda La Esmeralda.

The names of those lots have become a small genre. *Lychee Peach* (a Castillo), *Lychee* (Wush Wush), *Letty* (a Gesha named for a family member): they describe the cup more than the coffee, and they signal that the producer is staking everything on what the fermentation does, not on the variety. This is a deliberate inversion of the standard specialty narrative, where a rare variety carries the story.

Alex, the older brother, handles most of the farm operations and the human relationships — workers, neighbors, government inspectors. Diego runs the fermentation. The split isn't formal but it's clear from any visit. Where Alex is patient and outwardly calm, Diego is intense, and visibly bored by anything that isn't moving the chemistry forward. The Hachi Coffee Project, which the brothers co-founded with two other producer families in 2022, is partly a way to scale that intensity without scaling the farm: instead of growing more coffee themselves, they apply their processing techniques to lots from other Cauca producers, with attribution split. It is also, depending on who you ask, either a genuine attempt to lift the regional ecosystem or a vehicle for laundering the Bermudez brand into an indistinct collective.

Bermudez is the most controversial Colombian producer of the moment. In 2024 a lot entered into Best of Panama under the Hachi umbrella was disqualified after questions arose about variety lineage; the rumor in the industry was that the coffee had been altered through deliberate cross-pollination or grafting to behave more like a Gesha. The controversy didn't dent his commercial standing — buyers are still lining up — but it set off a real argument in the community about where exactly the line is between innovation and cheating. Diego has remained mostly quiet on the matter. The work continues.

His current primary importer is The Coffee Quest, which moves most of his volume into the U.S. and Europe. In NYC, Sey, Hydrangea, and Variety have all carried his lots; Onyx is a regular in the South. The farm sits only a few kilometers from Wilton Benitez's Granja Paraíso 92, and the two producers are alternately framed as rivals or as the two leads of a single regional school. From the outside they're the same story: chemists who happen to grow coffee.
