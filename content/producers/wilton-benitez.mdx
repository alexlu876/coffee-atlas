Wilton Benitez trained as a chemical engineer before turning to coffee. He runs three farms in Piendamó, Cauca — Granja Paraíso 92, La Macarena, and Las Brisas — and has built, on Granja Paraíso 92, a UV- and ozone-sterilized processing setup that looks more like a pharmaceutical clean room than a beneficio. The variety roster on the farms is unusual for Colombia: Sudan Rume, Eugenoides, Java, Wush Wush, Pink Bourbon, Chiroso, alongside the more conventional Castillo. La Macarena and Las Brisas are smaller — more experimental plots than commercial farms — and lots from them are sometimes blended with Granja Paraíso 92's, sometimes sold separately.

Where Bermudez's story tends to be told in microbiological terms — what specific yeast and bacteria do under what specific conditions — Benitez's reads as engineering. He is interested in *control*: removing every uncontrolled variable from the path between cherry and parchment. The cherries are sterilized before fermentation, killing the wild microbiome that would otherwise contribute to flavor. Specific cultures are introduced. The fermentation environment is monitored and adjusted. A thermal-shock step, similar in mechanism to Bermudez's but tuned differently, locks in volatiles. The drying is slow and uniform. The variation he wants in the cup is the variation he chose; the rest is noise to be eliminated.

The variety roster is what most distinguishes the farms from any other in the Cauca cluster. Sudan Rume and Eugenoides are barely grown commercially anywhere — the latter is technically not even *Coffea arabica* but a parent species, with tiny yields and an extreme floral character that can be hard to control. Benitez plants them anyway, in small experimental blocks, and processes them with the same precision as his Castillo.

The comparison to Bermudez is unavoidable. Both are in Piendamó. Both pioneered or refined fermentation techniques that have reshaped what Cauca coffee can taste like. Both are the kind of producer who would once have been a curiosity in a regional newsletter and is now competing for auction records against farms with century-long pedigrees. The difference is in temperament and approach: Bermudez optimizes for what the microbes can do given the right freedom; Benitez optimizes for what they will do given the right constraints. The cups, accordingly, can sit on opposite ends of a spectrum even when the variety and process are nominally the same.
