Wilton Benitez trained as a chemical engineer. He spent the early part of his career working in industrial fermentation — beer, mostly — and came back to coffee around 2015, on land his family had farmed in Piendamó for generations but had never tried to make competition-grade coffee from. Within five years he had three farms, a UV-and-ozone-sterilized processing setup that looked more like a pharmaceutical clean room than a beneficio, and a roster of varieties most Colombian producers had never planted: Sudan Rume, Eugenoides, Java, Wush Wush, Pink Bourbon, Chiroso. The flagship is Granja Paraíso 92, a few kilometers from Diego Bermudez's Finca El Paraíso. The other two, La Macarena and Las Brisas, are smaller — more experimental plots than commercial farms.

Where Bermudez's story is microbiological — what specific yeast and bacteria do under what specific conditions — Benitez's reads as engineering. He is interested in *control*: removing every uncontrolled variable from the path between cherry and parchment. The cherries are sterilized with ozone before fermentation, killing the wild microbiome that would otherwise contribute to flavor. Then specific cultures are introduced. The fermentation environment is monitored and adjusted. A thermal-shock step, similar in mechanism to Bermudez's but tuned differently, locks in volatiles. The drying is slow and uniform. The variation he wants in the cup is the variation he chose; the rest is noise to be eliminated.

The variety obsession is what most distinguishes the farms from any other in the Cauca cluster. Sudan Rume and Eugenoides are barely grown commercially anywhere — the latter is technically not even *Coffea arabica* but its parent species, and produces tiny yields with extreme floral character that can be hard to control. Benitez plants them anyway, in small experimental blocks, and processes them with the same precision as his Castillo. When a roaster carries a Benitez Eugenoides — Sey has had several — it sells out in hours, partly because of the variety's rarity and partly because his processing keeps the variety's character from collapsing into something either too fragile or too funky.

The comparison to Bermudez is unavoidable and frustrates both producers in different ways. Both are in Piendamó. Both pioneered or refined fermentation techniques that have reshaped what Cauca coffee can taste like. Both are the kind of producer who would once have been a curiosity in a regional newsletter and is now competing for auction records against farms with century-long pedigrees. The difference is in temperament and approach: Bermudez optimizes for what the microbes can do given the right freedom; Benitez optimizes for what they will do given the right constraints. The cups, accordingly, sit on opposite ends of a spectrum even when the variety and process are nominally the same.

Benitez sells primarily through The Coffee Quest, with smaller volumes moving through other importers as the farms scale up. His commercial spread in the U.S. is broader than Bermudez's — Onyx, Sey, Hydrangea, Black & White, and a dozen others have all carried Granja Paraíso 92 lots. He gives interviews more readily than Diego does, and has spoken in detail about his processing in a way that's allowed other Cauca producers to begin replicating elements of his approach. Whether that's good for the regional ecosystem or simply a slow process of homogenization is, like most things in this corner of specialty coffee right now, an open question.
