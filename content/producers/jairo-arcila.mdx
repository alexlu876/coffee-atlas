Jairo Arcila spent forty years as a mill manager at one of Colombia's largest coffee exporters, retiring in 2019. By the time he left, he had personally overseen the processing of more coffee than most countries produce in a year. He had also spent decades watching specialty buyers come through the mill and pick out the small percentage of lots that would command real prices, while the rest disappeared into commodity blends. The Arcila family farms — Esmeralda, Villarazo, Santa Mónica, Maracay, Buenos Aires, scattered around Armenia in Quindío — had been growing coffee for three generations, but had always sold it through that same anonymous channel.

What changed was Australia. In 2014 Jairo's sons, Carlos and Felipe, were studying and working in Melbourne. They walked into a third-wave café and saw a single-origin Colombian on the menu, traced (vaguely) to an estate not far from the family land, going for something like ten Australian dollars a cup. It was the kind of moment producers describe later as a *wait a minute*. They came back to Colombia with a plan, and in 2016 founded Cofinet, a green-coffee export company that would buy and represent specialty Colombian lots — including, increasingly, lots from their own father's farms.

Around the same period Jairo, anticipating retirement, was making a quieter move. In 2014, on the back of a bet that Gesha would become commercially viable in Colombia, he planted three thousand Gesha trees across the family farms. He was among the first ten Colombian producers to do this. The trees took the usual three to four years to bear meaningful fruit, and the early lots — processed by Cofinet, sold into Australia and the U.S. — were good enough to justify the bet. He has since planted Pink Bourbon, Chiroso, Java, Papayo, and Sidra; the farms now grow more named-variety blocks than most in the region, with Castillo and Caturra still doing the bulk of the volume.

Jairo's processing aesthetic is conspicuously restrained compared to the Cauca producers an hour to the south. Where Bermudez and Benitez run thermal-shock anaerobic ferments with bioreactors and ozone, Jairo and his daughter Marta do longer washed dwells, a careful natural program, and a process they call *Ice fermentation* — essentially a cold, slow anaerobic that produces a quieter, more layered cup than the Cauca pyrotechnics. The technique was co-developed with Cofinet and is now used by several Quindío producers in Cofinet's network. The implicit argument is that there are more roads up this mountain than the one going through Piendamó.

The family is part of the story in a way that's unusual at this tier. Jairo's wife, Luz Helena Salazar, was an accountant who took over farm management when Jairo was at the mill; she runs a separate set of farms, including La Leona, under her own name, and her lots ship through Cofinet too. Marta Arcila — Jairo's daughter — has another La Leona, distinct from her mother's, and is increasingly featured by Australian roasters. Carlos and Felipe operate Cofinet from Melbourne and Pereira respectively. None of this is presented as a brand strategy; it just happens to be what a long-running coffee family looks like in 2026.

Jairo himself is, by temperament, a quiet craftsman. He gives interviews in a slow, careful way and talks about coffee mostly in terms of what the trees and the climate are doing — varieties, altitudes, rainfall patterns — rather than in terms of process pyrotechnics. The contrast with the Cauca scene is not a reproach so much as a different model. The work has a forty-year horizon; the family makes decisions on the same scale.
