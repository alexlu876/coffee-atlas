Jairo Arcila spent forty years as a mill manager at one of Colombia's largest coffee exporters, retiring in 2019. He had spent decades watching specialty buyers come through the mill and pick out the small percentage of lots that would command real prices, while the rest disappeared into commodity blends. The Arcila family farms — Esmeralda, Villarazo, Santa Mónica, Maracay, Buenos Aires, scattered around Armenia in Quindío — had been growing coffee for three generations, but had largely sold it through that same anonymous channel.

What changed was Australia. Jairo's sons, Carlos and Felipe, were studying and working in Melbourne. They walked into a third-wave café, saw a single-origin Colombian on the menu traced — vaguely — to an estate not far from their family's land, and noticed the price. It was the kind of moment producers describe later as a *wait a minute*. They came back to Colombia with a plan, and founded Cofinet, a green-coffee export company that would buy and represent specialty Colombian lots — including, increasingly, lots from their own father's farms.

Around the same period Jairo, anticipating retirement, was making a quieter move. In 2014, on the back of a bet that Gesha would become commercially viable in Colombia, he planted three thousand Gesha trees across the family farms. He was among the first ten Colombian producers to do this. The trees took several years to bear meaningful fruit, and the early lots, processed and exported through Cofinet, were good enough to justify the bet. He has since planted Pink Bourbon, Chiroso, Java, Papayo, and Sidra; the farms now grow more named-variety blocks than most in the region, with Castillo and Caturra still doing the bulk of the volume.

Jairo's processing aesthetic is conspicuously restrained compared to the Cauca producers an hour to the south. Where Bermudez and Benitez run thermal-shock anaerobic ferments with bioreactors and ozone, Jairo and his daughter Marta favor longer washed dwells, careful naturals, and a process they call *Ice fermentation* — a cold, slow anaerobic that produces a quieter, more layered cup than the Cauca pyrotechnics. The technique was developed in collaboration with Cofinet and is now used by several Quindío producers in Cofinet's network. The implicit argument is that there are more roads up this mountain than the one going through Piendamó.

The family is part of the story in a way that's unusual at this tier. Jairo's wife, Luz Helena Salazar, was an accountant who took over farm management when Jairo was at the mill; she runs a separate set of farms — including her own farm called La Leona — under her own name, and her lots also ship through Cofinet. Marta Arcila has a separate farm of the same name (also called La Leona), distinct from her mother's, and is increasingly featured by Australian roasters. Carlos and Felipe run Cofinet's operations between Australia and Colombia. None of this is presented as a brand strategy; it just happens to be what a long-running coffee family currently looks like.

Jairo himself is, by temperament, a quiet craftsman. He gives interviews in a slow, careful way and talks about coffee mostly in terms of what the trees and the climate are doing — varieties, altitudes, rainfall patterns — rather than in terms of process pyrotechnics. The contrast with the Cauca scene is not a reproach so much as a different model. The work has a forty-year horizon; the family makes decisions on the same scale.
