Colombia is the world's third-largest coffee producer behind Brazil and Vietnam, but the only one of the three whose entire output is arabica. The country grows coffee along the spines of three Andean cordilleras, from Sierra Nevada de Santa Marta in the north to Nariño on the Ecuadorian border. The growing belt sits almost entirely between 1,200 and 2,300 meters; volcanic soil, year-round equatorial sun, and bimodal rainfall give most regions two distinct harvests and a long picking window overall.

The interesting story right now is not the volume but the experimentation. In a band running from Cauca through Quindío, a new generation of producers — Diego Bermudez, Wilton Benitez, the Arcila family — has spent the last fifteen years rebuilding what coffee fermentation can do. Bioreactors, thermal shock, ozone-sterilized cherries, hours-long anaerobic dwells: techniques that would have been laboratory exotica in 2010 are now what defines Colombian specialty.
